Gender Stereotypes in Comic Book Characters

An analysis of 34,476 comic book characters shows they propagate traditional, sometimes harmful gender roles

DISCLAIMER: All of the contents here are based on this story by Amanda Shendruk from The Pudding. Charts in this story are modified versions of the ones in Shendruk's article.

By MINAMI FUNAKOSHI

Published September 13, 2019

The 2017 release of Wonder Woman briefly catapulted the question of female superhero representation into the mainstream. For some, the character is a feminist icon — even Gloria Steinem wrote about her — and many fans (though not all) felt this wasn't just another superhero movie, but rather a pivotal moment in the portrayal of women in popular culture.

But the truth is that the comics industry has had a complicated relationship with female characters. They are often hyper-sexualized, unnecessarily brutalized, stereotyped, and used as tokens. They're also rare. Only 26.7 percent of all DC and Marvel characters are female, and only 12 percent of mainstream superhero comics have female protagonists.

Looking beyond the gender ratio, an analysis of how the characters are named what types of superpoweres they have shows that they reinforce harmful gender stereotypes.

Three out of five comic book characters have at least one superpower, regardless of gender. When we categorise these powers, we find that there are some clear gender imbalances.

Looking at the top traits and powers held by male characters, the message is clear: men are strong, invincible, and are born to lead. Here is a chart of selected superpowers that show how many male characters, compared to female ones, possess these highly physical powers.

The superpowers more commonly held by female characters reinforce the stereotype that women are nimble instead of strong, compassionate and thoughtful. They also reflect the stereotype of women as hysteric, sexually manipulative or controlling.

Pheromone control — the ability to generate and control pheromones that affect emotional and physical states, such as pleasure — occurs five times as often in a female character. Sonic scream appears in twice as many female characters as male; and prehensile hair — the ability to control one’s own hair and use it as a weapon that can bind up enemies — appears in female characters seven times more often.

Character monikers can be highly gendered, too. Consider some of the most popular comic characters of all time: Batman, Wonder Woman, Ms. Marvel and Spider-Man. Eight percent of both male and female characters have gendered names.

However, not all gendered names are equal. Women tend to have gendered titles that infantalise or sexualise them. Let's look at the percent of female and male characters with comparative gendered names. For example, we have paired “man” and “woman” as equivalent. Their usage, however, is not balanced: male characters are given “man” far more often than female characters are given “woman”.

To be fair, this problem does not affect comics alone: media in general — including children's literature, cartoons, computer games and even coloring books — have a history of poor female representation and gender imbalance. And often media reflect our world back to us: while only 27% of Marvel and DC characters are female, that number is only 19% for the United States Congress.

The good news is that things may be changing. Both DC and Marvel have slowly begun to increase the number of female characters in their output. Our analysis did not consider time-series data (we didn’t have it), and characters change over time — Wonder Woman herself has been a feminist activist, a house-wife, a sexualized pin-up, and a kick-ass hero in her 76 years. However, while some features may be altered, established and traditional characters tend to maintain their names and powers. They continue to carry the representative baggage of their past.
